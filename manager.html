    The moment they book, send the 3-minute "watch before our call" video. It pre-frames the opportunity, filters tire-kickers, and cuts Call 1 in half. The send-line — "if it's a clear no after watching, tell me and I'll free your slot" — filters for you before you spend a minute. → Script: PRECALL_VIDEO_SCRIPT.md (action: record it)

    Step 4 — The 3-Call Close

    Call Length Goal Do NOT

(proves their road won't get them there) 3. The Solution — Here's how this fixes THAT specific problem. 4. The Future — What does life look like once it's solved? (make them say it)

    Name: Family Shield Financial What we are: A nationwide (all-states) life insurance agency recruiting and developing licensed agents.     2. Who is a "good fit" — the criteria (written down, with teeth)

    You said "see if they'd be a good fit" — here is fit, defined. Call 2 exists to confirm these. Knockout questions (used by top FFL recruiters — short, scripted, intentional):

    Must-haves (knockouts — a "no" ends it): - [ ] Can fund the start: ~$200–500 for pre-licensing course + state exam + background check (and later, leads). "Are you willing to invest in yourself and fund your business?" - [ ] Time: can commit real hours, and can do Hell Week (10–12 hr days for one week). "Are you in a position to commit a full week of long days to launch?" - [ ] Coachable / accountable: "Are you the kind of person who can hold yourself accountable without a boss standing over you?" - [ ] A real WHY — a problem they're trying to solve (money, freedom, time, getting out of a job). No why = no fuel. - [ ] Clean enough background to be licensed in their state (felonies / certain financial crimes can block licensing). - [ ] Reliable phone + internet + willing to be on camera/calls.

    Green flags (lean in): sales/service/commission background, currently in pain with current job, has run out of options elsewhere, talks about family/people not just money, shows up early, asks good questions.

    Red flags (slow down or pass): wants guaranteed salary, can't fund the start, "I'll try it on the side and see," argues with the standard before signing, no why, flaky in scheduling.

    Top operators are explicit: recruiting is not convincing people to quit their job. It's asking simple questions that open a door, then letting the right ones walk through. You qualify hard so activation is easy.

    3. The discovery core (the heart of Call 2)

    This is the loop you described, formalized. On every fit call you are diagnosing, not pitching:

    1. The Problem — Why are you really on this call? What's not working where you are now?
    2. The History — What have you already tried or are doing now to fix that? (proves their current path won't get them there)
    3. The Solution — Here's specifically how this solves THAT problem.
    4. The Future — What does it look like once that problem is gone? — make them say it out loud.

    Find the problem → understand what they've done → show how you solve it → paint the solved future. (Full word-for-word version in CALL_SCRIPTS.md.)

    4. The Standard (set it before they sign, not after)

    The single most-skipped step. On Call 3, before signature, they hear exactly what's expected. People who hear the standard and still sign don't quit in week two. (Full version in STANDARDS_AND_AGREEMENT.md.)

    The standard, in short: - Get licensed in 10–14 days — exam scheduled on day one, no drifting. - Hell Week is non-negotiable: a full week of 10–12 hr days, first sale is the target. - Show up to every call and training. Do the activity. Run the script. - Chargeback & rollup terms understood and agreed (so nobody is surprised later). - We give world-class support; in return you give world-class effort.

    5. KPIs — what makes the system "VERY clear"

    You can't manage what you don't count. Track these weekly in RECRUIT_TRACKER.csv:

    Funnel metrics - DMs sent / day (target up to 50) - DM → reply % - Reply → Call 1 booked % - Call 1 → Call 2 % - Call 2 → signed % - No-show % (the silent killer — expect 30–50%; have a rebook cadence)

    Activation metrics - Signed → exam scheduled (should be ~100% within 48 hrs) - Exam scheduled → passed % - Passed → contracted % - Contracted → first sale within Hell Week % ← the number that actually matters

    If signed-but-no-sale is high, the leak is activation. If lots of DMs but few Call 1s, the leak is outreach/brand. The KPIs tell you which engine to fix.

    6. Follow-up & nurture (the leaky-bucket fix)

    • No-show on Call 1/2: same-day text → rebook. 3 touches over 5 days, then drop to long-term nurture.
    • "Not now": monthly check-in, keep them seeing content. Timing changes; the no isn't permanent.
    • Failed exam: don't release them — rebook the exam, reset study plan, keep momentum.
    • Everyone who ever engaged goes into the tracker. Nobody falls through.

    7. Compliance — non-negotiable rules (read this)

    Recruiting in this space draws FTC and state scrutiny. Bake these into every DM, call, and post:

    • No income promises. Never state or imply specific earnings ("make $10k/mo") in DMs, calls, or content. The FTC requires a reasonable basis for any income claim, and any earnings example must reflect typical results with a clear disclosure if it isn't. Safest path: don't make income claims at all — sell the system and opportunity, not a number.
    • Testimonials must be typical or carry a disclosure, and any paid/affiliated endorser's connection must be disclosed.
    • TCPA / DNC: recruiting outreach by phone/text must respect Do-Not-Call and consent rules. DM outreach to people who can opt out is lower-risk; cold-calling purchased lists is not — scrub against DNC. (Your dialer already enforces DNC + calling hours for customer leads — apply the same discipline to recruit outreach.)
    • PII / data security (GLBA / NAIC Model 668): recruit and client data must be stored securely. Don't keep SSNs or sensitive docs in plain spreadsheets.
    • Independent contractor framing: agents are 1099 independent contractors, not employees — keep "hire/employee/salary" language out of the agreement; use "contract/partner/agent."

    This file is operational guidance, not legal advice. Have a licensed attorney review the agent agreement and your income-claim language before scaling.

    📅 21-Day Schedule

    Family Shield Financial — The Schedule (Day-by-Day)

    The clock. From "signed agent agreement" (Day 0) to first sale. Target: licensed in 10–14 days, first sale by Day 21.

    The one lever that predicts everything: schedule the state exam on Day 0–1. A locked exam date with money on it is what turns "I'll study eventually" into "I test Thursday." Drifting on this is the #1 reason recruits stall. Set it first.

    All-states reality check (read once)

    You're recruiting nationwide, so the licensing path is not identical per recruit. Three things vary by state:

    Variable Range Examples
    Pre-licensing course hours 0 → 60 hrs TX, PA, AZ = 0 hrs (can test almost immediately) · IL = 40 · FL = 60 · CA = 12 hrs ethics (2026)
    Background check / fingerprints same-day → 1–2 weeks Some states clear instantly; others gate the license on prints
    State processing after exam hours → ~1 week License # may issue same day or take days

    What this means for the 10–14 day promise: the agent's part (study, pass) fits in ~7–10 days easily. The slippage is almost always state-side (prints/processing), not effort-side. Tell every recruit: "Your job is done in 10 days. The state's clock is the only thing that can add a few days — and we start it on day one so it never holds you up."

    Per-recruit, on Day 0 you look up their state's: required course hours, whether fingerprints are needed, and exam scheduling provider (Prometric / PSI / Pearson VUE). Put it in their tracker row. (Most carriers contract via SureLC; FFL-style onboarding triggers HCMS + SureLC invites.)

    The 14-day licensing sprint

    Day 0 — Sign day (same day as Call 3)

    • Welcome packet sent. Agent agreement signed (DocuSign or similar).
    • Look up their state requirements (hours, prints, provider) → tracker.
    • Schedule the state exam for ~Day 9–11. Pay/register now.
    • Enroll in pre-licensing course (state-approved; e.g. ExamFX / XCEL / Kaplan).
    • Start fingerprints/background check if their state requires it (do it TODAY — longest pole).
    • Send SureLC / contracting invite so carrier appointments run in parallel with studying.
    • Add to group chat / community. First win: they feel like they're already on the team.

    Day 1 — Orientation

    • 30-min onboarding call: walk the schedule, set the standard again, confirm exam date.
    • Give the study plan: daily course hours + practice-exam target (pass at 80%+ on practice before real exam).
    • Daily check-in cadence agreed (text or quick call every day).

    Days 2–8 — Study block

    • Course hours daily (front-load if their state requires many hours).
    • Practice exams starting Day 4. Track scores in tracker.
    • Daily accountability check-in. Watch for drift — if they miss a day, call, don't text.
    • Begin shadowing: have them listen to live sales calls now so the product makes sense before they're licensed.

    Days 9–11 — Pass

    • Sit the exam. (If practice scores aren't 80%+, push exam 2–3 days rather than burn the attempt — but only with cause, not drift.)
    • Passed → license # issued (timing per state).
    • If failed: rebook immediately, reset study plan, keep momentum. Do not release.

    Days 11–14 — Contract & arm

    • Confirm SureLC carrier appointments complete → writing numbers issued.
    • Set up: lead source, CRM, dialer access (your Family Shield dialer), scripts, calendar.
    • Final readiness check: can they log in, pull a lead, and dial? Do a live test dial together.
    • Green light for Hell Week.

    Hell Week (Days 14–21) — the activation crucible

    The point of Hell Week: compress a month of normal effort into 7 days so the new agent gets a first sale while motivation and support are at their peak. First money in week one is the single best predictor of retention.

    Daily structure (10–12 hr day): | Block | Hours | Activity | |---|---|---| | Morning huddle | 0.5 | Goals, role-play one objection, energy | | Dial block 1 | 3 | Live dials / appointments set | | Lunch + review | 1 | Listen back to a call, fix one thing | | Dial block 2 | 3 | Live dials / presentations | | Close support | 2–3 | You/mentor on the line for every close ("three-way close") | | Debrief | 0.5 | Numbers logged, tomorrow's plan, one lesson |

    Rules during Hell Week: - Every close is supported — new agent never closes alone in week one. - Numbers logged daily in the tracker (dials, contacts, appointments, presentations, sales). - One coaching point per day — not ten. Fix one thing at a time. - Goal: first sale by Day 21. Hitting it is the graduation moment.

    End of Hell Week: debrief, celebrate the first sale publicly (great content + recruiting proof), and roll them into the 30/60/90 plan.

    The referral ask (do it AT the graduation debrief, every time):

    "Who are 2–3 people in your world who'd want what you just did?"

    Ask it while the win is fresh — a brand-new agent with a first sale is your most credible recruiter, and warm referrals convert far better than cold DMs. Log the names in the tracker with Source = "Referral — [agent name]". This one question is what makes the machine self-feeding.

    After Hell Week — 30/60/90 (don't skip this)

    Your original plan ended at first sale. This is where chargebacks and quitters come from if you stop here.

    • Days 21–30: consistency — daily activity minimums, 2nd–4th sales, persistency habits (good business stays on the books = fewer chargebacks).
    • Days 30–60: independence — closing solo, building their own pipeline, first referrals.
    • Days 60–90: leadership track — top performers start learning to recruit. Your team compounds when agents become recruiters.

    📞 Call Scripts

    Family Shield Financial — Scripts (DM → 3-Call Close)

    Word-for-word frameworks. Don't read them robotically — internalize the intent of each step. The whole funnel is: attract → DM → Call 1 (intro) → Call 2 (fit/discovery) → Call 3 (offer).

    Compliance reminder on every line below: no income promises, no "make $X." Sell the opportunity, the system, and the support — never a number.

    A. Warm DM outreach (up to 50/day)

    Target: people who engage with your content + relevant non-followers. The signal that works is reference something specific about them — it triggers reciprocity and proves you're a human, not a blast.

    Opener (after they engage or follow):

    "Hey [name] — appreciate you following along. Quick one: are you in [their field] full-time right now, or open to other things?"

    If they reply / engaged with an AGENT or MENTOR post:

    "Saw you [liked the post about getting licensed / commented on the agent video]. Out of curiosity — what made that one catch your eye?"

    Bridge to a call (once there's a back-and-forth):

    "Honestly easier to explain on a quick call than type it all out. I do 10-min intro calls to see if it's even a fit both ways — want me to send a couple times?"

    DM rules: - Personalize the first line every time. No copy-paste blasts (kills reply rate + looks spammy). - One question per message. Make it easy to reply. - Goal of the DM is one thing: book Call 1. Don't pitch the business in the DMs. - Track every conversation in the tracker. Follow up twice if no reply, then let content keep warming them. - Up to 50/day, but personalized > volume. 30 real conversations beat 50 blasts.

    B. CALL 1 — Intro (5–10 min)

    Goal: explain what it is + light qualify + book Call 2. Do NOT close here. Top recruiters keep this to 5–7 minutes and intentional.

    1. Frame (30 sec)

    "Thanks for hopping on. This is a quick one — I just want to tell you what we actually do, ask you a couple things, and if it makes sense we'll set a longer call to go deeper. Fair?"

    2. What it is (1–2 min) — plain, no hype

    "We're Family Shield Financial. We help families protect their income with life insurance, and we build licensed agents to do that. It's a 1099 commission role — you get licensed, we hand you a proven system and leads, and we stay on the phone with you until you're making money. It's not a salary job and it's not for everyone — that's kind of the point of these calls."

    3. Light qualify — knockout questions (2–3 min)

    • "What's got you open to something new right now?" (listen for the WHY)
    • "Are you in a spot where you could invest a little in yourself to get licensed and started?"
    • "Are you someone who can hold themselves accountable without a boss on top of you?"

    4. Book Call 2 (1 min)

    "Here's how I run it — I don't offer anyone a spot on a first call. Next step is a longer call where we go deep on your situation and whether this actually solves something for you, and you grill me too. If after that we both feel it, there's a third call where I'd make you an offer. Sound fair? Let's get the next one on the calendar."

    If they're clearly unqualified (won't invest, wants salary, no why): be honest and kind — "I don't think this is the right fit, and I'd rather tell you straight." A clean no protects your time.

    C. CALL 2 — Fit / Discovery (the most important call, 20–40 min)

    Goal: run the discovery loop, confirm the fit criteria, get a mutual yes. This is where you find the problem and why they're on the call, exactly as you described. You talk ~30%, they talk ~70%.

    Step 1 — THE PROBLEM (why are they really here?)

    • "So tell me — what's actually going on that's got you looking? What's not working where you're at?"
    • "If nothing changed in the next year, where does that leave you?"
    • "What would have to be different for you to feel like you're winning?"

    Dig until you hit the real problem — money, time, freedom, dead-end job, providing for family. Write it down. This is the fuel for the whole journey.

    Step 2 — THE HISTORY (what have they tried / are doing?)

    • "What have you already tried to fix that?"
    • "What are you doing right now for money, and what's the ceiling on it?"
    • "Why hasn't [current path] gotten you there?"

    This proves to THEM that their current road won't reach their goal — without you having to say it.

    Step 3 — THE SOLUTION (connect it to THEIR problem)

    "Okay — based on what you just told me, here's specifically how this fixes that. You said [their problem]. With us, [the exact part of the system that solves it]. You get licensed in about two weeks, we hand you leads and a script, and we're on the phone with you for your first sales. That directly hits the [money/time/freedom] thing you described."

    Tailor it to what they said. Don't give the generic pitch — give the pitch shaped like their problem.

    Step 4 — THE FUTURE (make them say it)

    • "Let's say six months from now this worked. What's different in your life?"
    • "What does it mean for [their family / their why] if you pull that off?"

    When they describe the solved future in their own words, they've sold themselves. You just confirmed the fit.

    Step 5 — Confirm fit + tee up the offer

    "Honestly, based on everything — I think you could do this, and I think it solves the [problem] you came in with. The last step is a call where I lay out the actual offer, the agreement, and exactly what's expected on both sides. Before that call, I need you to be sure you want it, because when I make the offer I'm betting on you. Are you in for that conversation?"

    If they're not a fit, say so here. Better an honest no on Call 2 than a quitter in week two.

    D. CALL 3 — Offer + Set the Standard (15–30 min)

    Goal: make the offer, set the standard before signing, sign the agreement. (Full standard + agreement terms in STANDARDS_AND_AGREEMENT.md.)

    1. Recap their why (2 min)

    "Before anything — remind me why you're doing this." (Get them to restate the problem/why. Anchors the whole call in their motivation.)

    2. The offer (3 min)

    "Here's what I'm offering you: a spot as a licensed agent with Family Shield Financial. We get you licensed in 10–14 days, contract you with our carriers, hand you the system, leads, and a dialer, and we personally work your first sales with you in Hell Week. You're a 1099 partner — you own your business, we give you the machine and the mentorship."

    3. Set the standard — BEFORE the signature (5 min)

    "And here's what I expect in return, because I only bet on people who'll bet on themselves: - You schedule your exam in the next 48 hours and you get licensed in about two weeks. No drifting. - Hell Week is real — one week of 10–12 hour days. That's where your first sale comes from. Non-negotiable. - You show up to every call and training, you run the script, you do the activity. - You understand how chargebacks and rollup work (walk them through it now — see agreement) so nothing surprises you later. - In exchange, I give you everything I've got. I don't leave people hanging. Can you commit to that standard?"

    People who hear the standard and still say yes don't quit. People who flinch here were going to quit in week two anyway — better to find out now.

    4. Close / sign (5 min)

    "Then let's make it official." - Send the agreement (DocuSign). Sign on the call if possible. - Immediately: schedule the exam, send course + SureLC links, add to the team chat. - "Welcome to Family Shield. Day one starts now — let's get your exam booked before we hang up."

    E. Objection handling (quick reference)

    Objection Response (intent: empathize → reframe → re-ask)
    "I need to think about it." "Totally fair. Usually 'think about it' means there's one specific thing — is it the money to start, the time, or whether you can do it? Let's talk about that one."
    "I don't have the money to get licensed." "I hear you. The reason it costs a little is that it's a real license and a real business, not a gig. If [their why] matters enough, is the few hundred the real blocker, or is it something else?" (Don't fund them — qualify the commitment.)
    "Is this MLM / pyramid?" "No. You make money selling insurance to families who need it — full stop. You can build a team later if you want, but you eat off your own sales from week one."
    "I can only do it part-time." "A lot of people start while working. But Hell Week needs a full week of long days to get your first sale — can you carve that out? After that we talk schedule."
    "I've never done sales." "Most of our best agents hadn't. That's why there's a system and why we're on the calls with you. I'm not looking for experience, I'm looking for coachable and hungry — are you both?"
    "What if I fail?" "Then we rebook and go again — I don't release people who are putting in the work. The only way to fail is to quit on the activity."

    • Shawn Meaike on recruiting — "ask simple questions that open doors," don't convince people to quit their jobs.
    • Instagram DM reciprocity for recruiting — reference something specific; one ask per message; DMs/saves are the real signal.

    🤝 Standards & Agreement

    Family Shield Financial — Standards, Agreement & Onboarding

    What "setting the standard" actually contains, the agreement terms to cover (chargeback + rollup), and the onboarding checklist so nobody falls through.

    Not legal advice. Have a licensed attorney review the actual signed agreement and any income-claim language before you use it at scale.

    1. The Standard (the culture contract)

    Set this on Call 3 before signing, and repeat it at orientation. This is the bar that keeps quitters out and producers in.

    What we promise the agent: - Get you licensed in 10–14 days. - Contract you with strong carriers (competitive comp). - Hand you a proven system: leads, scripts, dialer, CRM. - Personal mentorship — we're on your first sales with you (Hell Week three-way closes). - Real support through chargeback/rollup issues, not abandonment.

    What the agent promises us: - Schedule the exam within 48 hours of signing. Licensed in ~2 weeks. No drifting. - Complete Hell Week — one week, 10–12 hr days. First sale is the target. - Attend every training and team call. Run the script. Hit daily activity minimums. - Protect the book: write quality business and serve clients so it stays on the books (persistency). - Coachable, accountable, honest. Bet on yourself the way we're betting on you.

    The deal is symmetrical: world-class support for world-class effort. State it that way.

    2. Agreement terms to cover (so nothing surprises them later)

    You specifically wanted the agreement to help with chargebacks and rollup. Cover these plainly on Call 3 — surprise on these is what creates resentment and quitting.

    Chargebacks — explain in plain English

    • Carriers often advance a chunk of the first-year commission up front (e.g., 6–9 months advanced).
    • If the policy cancels or lapses before that advance is "earned out," the carrier charges back the unearned portion — the agent owes it back.
    • How we protect the agent:
    • Train to write quality, affordable business the client will keep (right product, right budget) — the single biggest chargeback defense.
    • Teach persistency habits: confirm the draft date, do the welcome/retention call, make sure the first payment clears.
    • Be transparent about advance vs. as-earned options so a new agent can choose lower-risk as-earned while learning if they want.
    • A clear written policy on how team handles a chargeback balance (no hidden surprises).

    Rollup ("roll-up") — explain in plain English

    • In a hierarchy, if a downline agent leaves owing a chargeback balance the carrier can't collect, that debt can "roll up" to the upline who contracted them.
    • How we handle it as a team: explain it openly, set expectations about debt responsibility, and use it as the reason we hold a high standard at recruiting — we'd rather pass on a bad fit than carry their rollup. (This is also why the knockout questions and Hell Week exist: they prevent the situation that creates rollup.)

    Other agreement essentials

    • 1099 independent contractor status (not employee — no salary, no "hire" language).
    • Commission/comp level and advance terms in writing.
    • Lead cost responsibility (agent funds their leads).
    • Ownership of book / what happens if they leave.
    • Confidentiality + data handling (client PII, GLBA).
    • Compliance acknowledgment (no false income claims, DNC/TCPA, carrier rules).

    3. Onboarding checklist (Day 0 — run every time)

    Copy this per recruit into their tracker notes:

    [ ] Agreement signed (DocuSign)
[ ] State requirements looked up (course hours / fingerprints / exam provider)
[ ] State exam SCHEDULED (~Day 9–11) and paid
[ ] Pre-licensing course enrolled (state-approved provider)
[ ] Background check / fingerprints started (if state requires)
[ ] SureLC / contracting invite sent (carrier appointments)
[ ] Added to team group chat / community
[ ] Orientation call booked (Day 1)
[ ] Daily check-in cadence agreed
[ ] Study plan + practice-exam target (80%+) given
[ ] Shadowing scheduled (listen to live calls during study week)

    Then before Hell Week:

    [ ] Exam PASSED, license # recorded
[ ] Carrier appointments complete, writing numbers issued
[ ] Lead source set up
[ ] CRM access
[ ] Dialer access (Family Shield dialer) + test dial done together
[ ] Scripts delivered, one role-play completed
[ ] Hell Week dates confirmed on calendar

    4. Income-claim safe language (use everywhere)

    Instead of numbers, say: - "Commission-based — what you earn depends on your effort and results." - "We give you the system and the support; the work is on you." - "Top agents do well, but results vary and aren't guaranteed."

    Never: specific dollar figures, "replace your income in X weeks," "guaranteed," ranking screenshots without a typical-results disclosure. (FTC: any income claim needs a reasonable basis and must reflect typical results.)

    ⏰ Daily Rhythm

    Family Shield Financial — Your Daily Operating Rhythm

    The schedule covers the recruit's 21 days. This covers your day — because the 50 DMs and the check-ins only happen if they have a time slot, not a "when I get to it."

    Adjust the clock times to your life; keep the blocks and their order. The order matters: outreach happens before the day can eat you.

    The daily blocks

    Block Time What happens Non-negotiable output
    1. DM Power Hour 8:00–9:00a New outreach: reply to overnight engagement first (hottest leads), then new personalized DMs DMs sent + every convo logged in tracker
    2. Pipeline sweep 9:00–9:30a Tracker top-to-bottom: today's calls confirmed, no-show touches sent, study check-in texts out Zero rows untouched > 48 hrs
    3. Call block 10:00a–12:30p Call 1s / Call 2s / Call 3s, stacked back-to-back (energy compounds) Calls done + tracker updated between calls, not "later"
    4. Your own production 1:30–4:30p Your sales / Hell Week support / three-way closes with new agents New agents never close alone
    5. Evening sweep 7:00–8:00p Second DM pass (evening reply rates are best), study-window check-in replies, tomorrow's call list Tomorrow already scheduled before you sleep
    6. Score the day 8:00–8:15p Log the day's numbers (below). 5 minutes, every day The scoreboard row filled in

    During an active Hell Week, block 4 expands and block 3 shrinks — Hell Week agents outrank new recruiting calls. One first-sale agent recruits better than five cold DMs.

    The daily scoreboard (15 numbers max, 5 minutes)

    Keep a running sheet (or add a tab to the tracker). Per day:

    DMs sent | Replies | Calls booked | C1 done | C2 done | C3 done | Signed
No-shows | Exams scheduled | Exams passed | Agents in study | Agents in Hell Week | Sales (team) | Sales (yours)

    The weekly review (Sunday, 30 minutes)

    Once a week, compute the funnel and find THE leak — fix one thing per week, not five:

    1. Funnel math: DM→reply %, reply→booked %, booked→C1 show %, C1→C2 %, C2→signed %.
    2. Activation math: signed→exam scheduled %, exam→passed %, passed→first sale %.
    3. Find the worst number vs last week. That's the only thing you change this week (script tweak, template tweak, more volume — one lever).
    4. Pipeline forecast: how many in each stage right now → how many agents land in Hell Week in the next 2 weeks?
    5. Content check: did this week's AGENT/MENTOR posts go out? (Top of funnel starves silently — see CONTENT_PLAN.)

    Weekly volume targets (starting point — recalibrate after 30 days of data)

    Metric Target/week
    DMs sent 150–250 (quality > the 50/day cap)
    Call 1s held 8–12
    Call 2s held 4–6
    Signed 1–2
    In Hell Week 1–2 at any time

    If signed ≥ 2/week consistently → switch to cohort onboarding (start them together, shared Hell Week, class group chat). Peer accountability replaces half your check-in texts.

    Rules that keep the rhythm honest

    • Outreach before admin. The DM hour is first because it's the easiest thing to skip and the most expensive to skip.
    • Tracker updates happen in the moment, between calls. A stale tracker lies, and you'll trust it next week.
    • Protect the call block. No errands, no editing videos inside it. Calls book against a calendar that actually exists.
    • One coaching point per agent per day. Same rule as Hell Week — applies to you coaching everyone.
    • Sunday review is sacred. It's the only place the system improves instead of just runs.

    📖 Glossary

    Plain-English definitions of the terms a brand-new agent needs to know.

    Chargeback
    Carriers often pay part of a policy's first-year commission up front (an advance). If the client cancels before that's 'earned out,' the unearned part is taken back — that's a chargeback. Writing affordable business clients keep is the best defense.
    Rollup
    If a downline agent leaves owing a chargeback the carrier can't collect, that debt can 'roll up' to the agent who contracted them. It's the main reason we recruit to a high standard.
    Persistency
    The share of your policies that stay active and paid over time. High persistency = fewer chargebacks and a healthier book.
    Advance vs as-earned
    Advance = paid a chunk of commission up front (faster money, chargeback risk). As-earned = paid as the client pays their premiums (slower, lower risk). New agents can choose lower-risk as-earned while learning.
    SureLC
    The online system most carriers use to contract (appoint) agents. Runs in parallel with studying so you can write business the day you pass.
    Writing number
    The ID a carrier issues once you're contracted — it's how your business and commissions get tracked to you.
    NIPR
    National Insurance Producer Registry — where licenses are applied for and tracked across states.
    PLE
    Pre-Licensing Education — the course hours some states require before you can sit the exam. Ranges from 0 to 60+ hours by state (see State Lookup).
    IMO
    Independent Marketing Organization — the distribution structure agencies plug into to access carriers and comp. Family Shield operates in this model.
    1099
    You're an independent contractor, not an employee — you own your business, set your hours, and earn commission (no salary).
    Hell Week
    Your launch week: one focused week of long days on the phones with a mentor on your closes, aimed at landing your first sale fast.
    DNC / TCPA
    Do-Not-Call and the Telephone Consumer Protection Act — the rules governing who you can call/text and how. We scrub and respect them.
